Naga shawls

Naga shawls, a vibrant tradition in Nagaland, India, are handwoven and symbolize cultural identity, status, and achievement. The shawls are traditionally made using a backstrap loom, with natural dyes and hand-spun yarn. They are woven in distinctive patterns and colors, often reflecting the specific tribe and wearer's social standing.

Loin Loom

The loin loom, also known as a backstrap loom, is one of the oldest and simplest weaving methods, used by indigenous communities around the world. It's characterized by a strap that's worn around the weaver's waist to maintain tension on the warp threads. The weaving process involves using a series of bamboo sticks and a strap to create the warp, followed by inserting the weft threads to create the fabric.

Ao naga

The Ao Naga tribe has a rich history and tradition of weaving, with women playing a central role in the art. Weaving is believed to have originated with a woman named Longkongla, who supposedly had magical powers and initiated the practice to differentiate clans.
